# The 8 Essential Rules for Naming

Based on Lexicon Branding's methodology and David Placek's published rules. Every name generated by this skill must be checked against these 8 rules. Violation of any rule should downgrade or eliminate the name, not just be noted.

---

## Rule 1: The Name Must Be Distinctive

Not just different — distinctive. It should stand out in the category AND be easy to find.

**Test:** Search the name alongside the product category in a search engine. If brand X in the same space shows up first, the name isn't distinctive enough.

**Common violation:** Using generic category words ("Smart," "Cloud," "Data," "Web," "Tech," "Net," "App," "AI," "GPT"). These are not distinctive.

**Exception:** When the brand is SO dominant it redefines the word (Google as a verb, Amazon as a store). But this is after years of investment — not a naming strategy.

---

## Rule 2: Semantic Emptiness Is An Asset

Names that carry too much literal meaning limit the brand's future. A name that means exactly what the product does today can't expand tomorrow.

**Test:** Could this brand sell something completely different in 5 years?

**Bad:** "BookWorld" — can't expand beyond books. "TaxiApp" — can't expand beyond taxis. "BurgerOnly" — limited.

**Good:** Amazon (books → everything), Apple (computers → phones → services → entertainment), Nike (shoes → athletic anything).

**Lexicon's principle:** "Great names are vessels into which meaning can be poured. The name does not deliver the meaning — the brand experience does."

---

## Rule 3: Short Is King

Two to three syllables. Two is ideal. Four is acceptable if the name is otherwise perfect. Five or more is almost never appropriate for a modern brand.

**Test:** Can the name fit comfortably in a mobile notification? A tweet? A voice command?

**Why this matters:** The brain processes shorter words with less cognitive load. Short names are:
- Easier to say in conversation
- More likely to be remembered
- Faster to type
- Less error-prone in voice interfaces
- Better for global pronunciation

**Examples:** Nike (1), Zoom (1), Uber (2), Apple (2), Tesla (2), Sonos (2), Vercel (2), Swiffer (2), Google (2), Amazon (3), Instagram (3), Microsoft (3). Notice: Instagram is 3 syllables but dominant because it was category-defining.

---

## Rule 4: Sound Symbolism Must Support the Strategy

The sounds in the name must reinforce the emotional territory of the brand. This is not optional — it's the primary filter.

**Test:** Does saying the name out loud evoke the emotion the brand wants to own?

**Example mapping:**
- A security company should NOT use liquid/glide-heavy names ("Lulumi," "Waverly") — sounds soft, not strong
- A luxury brand should NOT use front vowel + plosive blasts ("Tick," "Kip") — sounds cheap and fast
- A children's app should NOT use back vowel + dense consonants ("Throm," "Gond") — sounds heavy and scary
- A fintech should NOT use sibilant-heavy names ("Sashy," "Shilo") — sounds too soft for trust and speed

See `sound-symbolism.md` for the full phoneme-to-perception mapping.

---

## Rule 5: The Name Must Work in the "Oral Culture"

People hear names before they see them. If the name is hard to spell from hearing, or ambiguous when spoken, it fails.

**Test:** Say the name to 5 people. Can they spell it? Can they Google it without clarification?

**Common violations:**
- Unusual spellings meant to be clever ("Flickr" — people type "Flicker")
- Homophones with common words ("Bare" vs "Bear," "Rite" vs "Right")
- Silent letters that create confusion ("Phreedom," "Knicce")
- Hyphens or special characters in the core name ("A-Book," "Fl!p")

**Good:** Google is phonetically obvious. Uber is unambiguous. Sonos sounds like it's spelled.

---

## Rule 6: Meaning Should Not Be Negative in Any Major Language

One language's beautiful word is another's insult, joke, or profanity. Every generated name must pass a basic cross-cultural sniff test.

**Test:** Does it sound like any of the following in Spanish, French, German, Italian, Portuguese, Mandarin Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Arabic, Hindi?
- A body part, bodily function, or toilet reference
- A common profanity or slur
- A derogatory term for a group
- A vulgar verb or noun
- A phrase with negative idiomatic meaning

**Real cases:**
- "Mist" in German means "manure"
- "Pinto" in Brazilian Portuguese is slang for male genitalia
- "Nova" in Spanish => "no va" = "doesn't go" (urban legend but widely believed)
- (Unverified) "Lumia" — reportedly has slang issue in Spanish (urban legend); do not rely on this without independent linguistic confirmation.
- "Barf" in parts of the Middle East means "snow" — fine there, funny in English

**Note:** The skill cannot do formal trademark or linguistic clearance. It flags HIGH PROBABILITY risks based on sound similarity and common words. Always recommend professional linguistic vetting before launch.

---

## Rule 7: A Name Must Create Positive Engagement

The name alone should make people lean in, not away. It should invite curiosity or confidence.

**Test:** What's the first emotion someone feels upon hearing it? Boredom? Confusion? Mild disgust? That's a failure.

**Positive engagement comes from:**
- Surprising combinations (unexpected compounds)
- Familiar-yet-new neologisms
- Names that suggest possibility, not limitation
- Names that sound like someone you'd trust or want to know

**Negative engagement signals:**
- Blandness (makes the brand invisible)
- Complexity (makes people feel stupid)
- Vagueness (makes people wonder what it is AND not care enough to find out)
- Cynicism (suggests the brand is hiding something)

---

## Rule 8: The Name is Not the Brand — But It Opens the Door

A great name cannot save a bad product. But a bad name can kill a good product by making it invisible, confusing, or off-putting.

**The naming job is:** To create a doorway. The product, marketing, and experience must furnish the room.

**Test:** If the brand succeeds, will the name feel like it "deserves" the success? Or will people think "great product, weird name?"

**Examples:**
- "BackRub" was Google's original name. It described the product perfectly but had zero brand potential.
- "Cadabra" was Jeff Bezos's almost-name for Amazon. Sounded like "cadaver."
- "Relentless" was an Amazon domain Bezos owned — redirects to Amazon to this day.

Even the best founders get this wrong without help. The skill exists precisely because naming is hard and high-stakes.

## The "No-Go" List — Names to Never Generate

These patterns are overused, dated, or ineffective. If any generated name matches these patterns, discard it and regenerate:

1. **The "-ify" suffix** (except Spotify, which is the ONLY exception and it succeeded despite this)
2. **The "-ly" suffix** (Bit.ly era — now feels like 2010)
3. **The "me" suffix** (Vimeo, Resume — useful at a point in time, now feels Myspace-era)
4. **"App" anywhere in the name** ("WeatherApp," "NotesApp" — this was wrong in 2010 and is worse now)
5. **"AI" or "GPT" as prefix/suffix** unless the brand IS the AI model itself. For a general tool, this dates in 12-18 months.
6. **Generic tech compounds** ("TechFlow," "ByteWave," "CloudSync," "DataNest," "NetSpark")
7. **Greek letter soup** ("Theta," "Lambda," "Sigma") — awash in crypto/science projects, indistinctive
8. **Abstract "sh" sound clusters for every name** — AI loves "Shila," "Shomo," "Shyft" — they're all forgettable
9. **Random syllable smash** ("Zybno," "Flarp," "Krint") — no sound symbolism, no structural logic, no meaning
10. **Too cute for the context** — "SnuggleCloud" for enterprise security, "CuddlyData" for anything
