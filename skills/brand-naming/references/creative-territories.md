# Creative Territories

Every naming run generates names across these five territories. The model selects which territories to emphasize based on the strategic brief. Not all territories are always relevant.

---

## 1. Invented / Neological

Names that feel like real words but are constructed. The most valuable territory for trademarkability and distinctiveness.

**How to generate:**
1. Start with a root concept from the brief (speed, security, flow, warmth, precision)
2. Find a language root or syllable that maps to that concept
3. Add or subtract endings until it feels natural
4. Test: Does it pass the "is this a real word?" test — should feel like it COULD be a word

**Successful examples:**
- **Sonos** — from "sonic," with palindromic symmetry. Pure sound symbolism over semantics.
- **Vercel** — short, punchy, two syllables, front vowel + liquid + plosive. Suggests velocity and precision.
- **Swiffer** — from "swipe" + "sweep." Sibilant-heavy, suggesting smooth, fast cleaning.
- **Febreze** — from "fabric" + "breeze." Back vowel opening to sibilant closure — freshness and air.
- **Pentium** — Greek "penta" + scientific "-ium." Created a product category feeling (chips as elements).
- **Dasani** — from "sanskrit water" root + soft vowels. Pure liquid hydration feeling.
- **Impossible** — not a neologism, but recontextualized as a brand name — shifts from negative to aspirational.
- **Lucid** — real word, but in the EV context becomes fresh and futuristic.

**Generator heuristics:**
- Take a relevant word, remove the first or last syllable, add a new one
- Combine two relevant words but blend at the syllable level (not just concatenation)
- Invent using sound symbolism alone: pick the emotional target, pick the phoneme, build outward
- Use scientific/latin/greek suffixes (-ium, -on, -is, -ent, -ic) to suggest authority
- Use diminutive/enlarging suffixes (-let, -ie, -o, -a) to suggest scale

## 2. Unexpected Compounds

Two words that don't naturally go together — the friction creates memorability. The best compounds surprise the listener into paying attention.

**How to generate:**
1. List nouns, verbs, and adjectives related to the product space
2. List nouns, verbs, and adjectives from OPPOSITE semantic fields
3. Force pairings that create conceptual tension
4. Check: Does the compound create a new meaning neither word has alone?

**Successful examples:**
- **BlackBerry** — a dark fruit + a tech device. Playful, memorable, organic inside technology.
- **PowerBook** — power + book. Suggests portable strength and knowledge.
- **Impossible Burger** — impossible + meat. Provokes curiosity and redefinition.
- **Cloudflare** — cloud (soft, diffuse, distant) + flare (bright, sudden, warning). Suggests a sudden light in the fog — protection making the invisible visible.
- **SoundCloud** — sound + cloud. A cloud for sound — naturally metaphorical.
- **Instagram** — instant + telegram. Fast communication, but visual.
- **FireFox** — fire (danger, speed, warmth) + fox (clever, quick). A browser with personality.

**Generator heuristics:**
- Take a functional word from the category and pair it with something organic/natural
- Take an organic word and pair it with something technical
- Combine opposing qualities: fast/slow, hard/soft, light/heavy, old/new
- Use color, animal, or elemental words as anchors (they carry rich symbolic weight)

## 3. Evocative Real Words

Existing words used metaphorically, not literally. The brand recontextualizes the word, owning new territory.

**How to generate:**
1. List emotional qualities or behaviors the brand wants to own
2. Find words that PERSONIFY those qualities in non-brand contexts
3. Check: Is the word broad enough to carry the brand's future, not just its product today?

**Successful examples:**
- **Apple** — healthy, accessible, knowledge (from Newton's apple, Garden of Eden). Recontextualized technology.
- **Amazon** — vast, powerful, flowing, natural. Recontextualized commerce.
- **Oracle** — wisdom, foresight, ancient authority. Recontextualized enterprise software.
- **Medium** — a channel, a middle ground, accessible. Recontextualized publishing.
- **Stripe** — a clean, sharp line. Recontextualized payments (clean, simple).
- **Raven** — intelligence, black, otherworldly. Works for AI, security, creative tools.
- **Atlas** — strength, holding up the world, maps. Works for infrastructure, maps, heavy-duty anything.
- **Chrome** — shiny, fast, reflective. Became a verb.

**Generator heuristics:**
- Look for words with rich symbolic/connotative history (mythology, nature, emotions, physics)
- Favor words with dual meanings or metaphorical flexibility
- Test against "could this be in the dictionary without the brand?" — the broader the word, the bigger the brand can grow
- Avoid words that have strong negative connotations in any culture

## 4. Sound-Symbolic Constructions

Names engineered primarily for how they hit the ear. Semantics are secondary or absent — the meaning IS the sound.

**How to generate:**
1. Define the emotional/acoustic target from the brief (smooth, fast, heavy, sharp, warm)
2. Pick the core phoneme cluster that matches that target
3. Build a pronounceable word around that cluster
4. Test: Does saying it out loud create the intended feeling?

**Successful examples:**
- **Turo** — Short, back vowel, liquid closure. Suggests smooth, confident, international. Easy in every language.
- **Civic** — Soft 'c's, short i, approachable. Suggests community, roundness, reliability.
- **Verizon** — Latin "veritas" (truth) + "horizon." But mostly works because the 'z' and 'n' give it propulsion.
- **Acura** — Precision-formed, front vowel + liquid. Suggends exactness, Japanese engineering aesthetic.
- **Jaguar** — Liquid + affricate + liquid. Growls when you say it.
- **Kodak** — Invented by George Eastman specifically for sound: "a strong, incisive name." K + short o + d + short a + k. Plosive bookends, vowel body.

**Generator heuristics:**
- Start with a phoneme, not a concept. "I want warmth" → start with nasal + back vowel (mo-, no-, om-)
- Use repeated consonants for rhythm (Tala, Kiki, Lulu, Momo)
- Use plosive-sibilant contrast for energy (K + s, T + sh, P + z)
- Test in multiple accents — some sounds don't travel (e.g. 'th' is hard in many languages)

---

## 5. Abstract / Visual / Structural

Names that work through structure, visual appearance, or cognitive patterns rather than sound or meaning alone.

**How to generate:**
1. What structural quality could make the name distinctive? Palindrome? Three identical letters? Upside-down symmetry?
2. What visual quality matters? All caps? Short letterforms? Ascenders/descenders?
3. Can the name work as both a word and a shape?

**Successful examples:**
- **Sonos** — Palindromic structure (reads same upside down). O is a circle — suggests completeness, sound waves, unity.
- **WW** — Two letters, visually solid, infinitely scalable. Replaced "Weight Watchers" entirely.
- **Häagen-Dazs** — Invented foreignness (sounds Scandinavian, isn't). Uses umlaut + Z for visual distinctiveness.
- **C++** / **F#** / **R** — Programming languages named with symbols. The visual IS the name.
- **Odeo** — Repeating O, palindromic structure. Memorable for podcasting (round sound).

**Generator heuristics:**
- Test the name in all lowercase, all caps, and title case — does it look like itself in all forms?
- Consider letters with strong visual identity: O, X, Z, I, M, W, S
- Palindromes are rare but powerful: think in terms of letter symmetry
- Abbreviating or removing vowels can create modern tech feel (FLX, NTWRK) but risks illegibility

## Territory Selection Matrix

| Brief Signal | Emphasize | Downweight |
|---|---|---|
| Tech / SaaS / AI | Invented, Sound-Symbolic, Abstract | Evocative Real Words |
| Consumer / Food / Wellness | Neological, Evocative, Unexpected Compounds | Abstract |
| Enterprise / B2B | Sound-Symbolic, Unexpected Compounds, Evocative | Neological (unless made-up sounds authoritative) |
| Creative / Design / Fashion | Abstract, Sound-Symbolic, Evocative | Sound-Symbolic (if too clinical) |
| Mobile / App-first | Short (1-2 syllables), Invented, Sound-Symbolic | Longer compounds |
| Global / International | Short, Sound-Symbolic, Abstract (avoid real-word culture-lock)| Evocative with local meanings |

## Tagline Patterns

Once a name is chosen, taglines should reinforce its emotional territory:

| Name Territory | Tagline Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Invented/Tech | "X for Y" or capability claim | "Vercel — Develop. Preview. Ship." |
| Unexpected Compound | Resolve the tension or celebrate it | "The browser that bites back" (Firefox) |
| Evocative Real Word | Extend the metaphor | "Think different" (Apple) |
| Sound-Symbolic | Short, rhythmic, alliterative | "Can you hear me now?" (Verizon era) |
| Abstract/Visual | Minimalist, confident | "Just do it" (Nike — works for any abstract name) |
